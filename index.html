Bakra Eid

Celebrate the Spirit of Sacrifice, Faith, and Togetherness. Bakra Eid, officially known as Eid al-Adha (the Festival of Sacrifice), is one of the most important and sacred festivals in Islam. It is celebrated by millions of Muslims worldwide to honor a profound story of faith, devotion, and obedience to God.

Why is it celebrated? (The History)

The roots of this festival go back to the Prophet Ibrahim (Abraham). According to Islamic tradition, God commanded Prophet Ibrahim in a dream to sacrifice his beloved son, Ismail, as a test of his faith. Displaying ultimate devotion, Ibrahim agreed to follow God's command. His son, Ismail, also willingly accepted. However, just as Ibrahim was about to perform the sacrifice, God intervened and replaced Ismail with a ram, sparing the boy's life. This event proved Ibrahim's absolute love and submission to God, and Bakra Eid is celebrated every year to commemorate this ultimate act of faith.

How is it celebrated? (The Traditions)

Today, Muslims recreate this act of devotion through Qurbani, which means sacrificing a livestock animal—usually a goat (bakra), sheep, cow, or camel. The celebration is built on beautiful principles of charity and community: The Three Parts: The meat from the sacrificed animal is divided equally into three shares. One part goes to the home and family, the second part is gifted to relatives and friends, and the crucial third part is given entirely to the poor and needy. This ensures that absolutely no one in the community goes hungry during the festival. Ultimately, Bakra Eid is not just about the sacrifice of an animal; it is a time for self-reflection. It teaches Muslims the values of selflessness, gratitude, charity, and the importance of helping the less fortunate while strengthening the bonds of brotherhood and unity.
